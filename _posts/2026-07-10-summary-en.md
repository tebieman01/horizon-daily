## [Quantum Koopman Method Simulates Nonlinear Dynamics on 10 Qubits](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07338) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Researchers at Peking University proposed the quantum Koopman method, a data-driven framework that embeds nonlinear dynamics into a learned linear representation and implements it using shallow quantum circuits. They demonstrated it on a superconducting processor with up to 32 parallel circuits of 10 qubits, simulating reaction-diffusion, fluid motion on a sphere, and Gulf Stream currents. This work provides a hardware-validated route for simulating moderately nonlinear dynamics on near-term quantum hardware, overcoming the fundamental limitation of unitary quantum evolution. It identifies a practical boundary for quantum-amenable nonlinear dynamics, with potential impact on quantum computing and nonlinear dynamics. The method learns Koopman observables from trajectory data, projects the lifted dynamics onto a finite-dimensional subspace, and decomposes the non-unitary propagator into parallel spectral channels. The experiments revealed a transition from performance limited by hardware noise in weakly nonlinear systems to performance limited by finite-dimensional Koopman representations as nonlinear scale interactions increase.

rss · ArXiv - cs.AI · Jul 9, 04:00

**Background**: Nonlinear dynamics is ubiquitous in nature but simulating it on quantum computers is fundamentally limited by the unitary nature of quantum evolution. The Koopman method is a data-driven approach that transforms nonlinear dynamics into linear evolution in a higher-dimensional observable space, making it amenable to quantum simulation. Superconducting quantum processors are a leading platform for quantum computing, using superconducting circuits as qubits.

Anthropic researchers developed the Jacobian lens, a technique that reads out what internal activations in Claude are disposed to make the model say, providing the clearest view yet of how LLMs process concepts. They also introduced the Bag of Dims framework, showing that individual dimensions in transformer hidden states act as binary registers encoding semantic content via sign patterns. This breakthrough in AI interpretability allows researchers to directly observe and steer concepts inside LLMs without training additional probes, potentially leading to safer and more controllable AI systems. The finding that sign patterns alone carry predictive content and are causally operative opens new avenues for understanding and debugging model behavior. The Jacobian lens linearly transports a residual-stream vector at any layer and position into the final-layer basis, then decodes it with the model's own unembedding into a ranked list of vocabulary tokens. The Bag of Dims framework was validated across seven models spanning language, vision, and audio, and shows that a full-capacity MLP adds zero AUC over per-dimension reading.

**Background**: Large language models (LLMs) like Claude are often treated as black boxes because their internal representations are difficult to interpret. Traditional interpretability methods rely on training separate probes or analyzing attention patterns, which can be costly and may not capture the full picture. The Jacobian lens and Bag of Dims offer training-free, architecture-general approaches that leverage the model's own weights to read and steer concepts directly from hidden states.

Researchers introduced Hamiltonian Replica Exchange Transition Interface Sampling (HRETIS), a path sampling framework that exchanges Hamiltonians between path ensembles to enhance exploration of rare events in complex energy landscapes. HRETIS addresses a key challenge in computational chemistry and physics: sampling rare events like drug binding in systems with multiple pathways separated by orthogonal barriers, potentially accelerating drug discovery and materials design. The method is demonstrated on coarse-grained simulations of amino acid permeation through a DPPC membrane, showing improved sampling efficiency and convergence compared to standard approaches.

rss · ArXiv - q-bio.QM · Jul 9, 04:00

Path sampling methods like transition interface sampling (TIS) and replica exchange transition interface sampling (RETIS) are used to study these events by focusing on the transition paths. HRETIS combines these ideas to enhance decorrelation in path space.

EFSA has recommended this approach for pesticide risk assessment.

Researchers developed a triple-phase multimodal framework that combines slit-lamp images under three illumination conditions with clinical metadata to classify bacterial versus fungal keratitis, achieving 85.84% accuracy and 0.885 AUC on a multicenter dataset of 1,645 patients.
